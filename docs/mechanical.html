Requirements & Constraints

Puck Specifications

  • Material: hardened steel chrome with ABS plastic top cover
  • Diameter: 2.08 inches
  • Thickness: 1 inch
  • Can be slid on either the steel chrome face or colored plastic face
  • Weight: 8 oz.

 

DROID Constraints

  • Maximum footprint in starting zone: 13 in × 13 in
  • Maximum footprint out of starting zone: 20 in × 20 in
  • Maximum height: 12 in
  • Must contain all pucks internally (fits in the DROID footprint)
  • Front of DROID, as defined by DROID dimensions in the starting zone, cannot cross the hog line by over a centimeter.

Chassis

The chassis consists of two levels to stay within size constraints and to accommodate the puck storage and shooting mechanism.

Bottom level:

  • Flywheel shooting mechanism
  • Wheels, motors, and respective motor drivers
  • Line sensing
  • Batteries

Upper level:

  • Beacon sensing circuit
  • Puck storage and servo
  • Arduino Mega and peripherals (voltage regulators, servo motor driver)
  • Barrier terminal block for power distribution

The two levels are connected by standoffs, and the ramp from the puck storage extends down to the lower level flywheel.

Wheels and Driving

We picked mecanum wheels so we didn't have to rotate the robot in order to turn. These wheels allow the robot to move sideways without rotating, and the direction of motion is controlled by the software.

We used single shear mounting on motors to stay within size constraints. We used high-grade fasteners and PLA-reinforced mounts to handle the radial loads.

Puck Shooting Mechanism

The shooting assembly is powered by a 30W DC motor driving a high-friction 4.8-inch OD wheel.

To achieve an 8-foot range, we implemented a software-based "boost" phase. Immediately after a puck is detected leaving the chamber, the motor is pulsed to 100% duty cycle to rapidly recover the RPM lost to kinetic energy transfer before the next puck is pushed through.

Motor Specification Calculations

1. Vertical Kinematics ($y$-direction)

Determine time of flight based on the maximum allowed puck height of 1 inch:

$$v_f^2 = v_i^2 + 2ad$$

Given $v_i = 0$, $a = 9.81 \, \text{m/s}^2$, $d = 1 \, \text{in} = 0.0254 \, \text{m}$:

$$v_f = \sqrt{2(9.81)(0.0254)} = 0.7059 \, \text{m/s}$$

Time of flight from $v_f = v_i + at$:

$$t = \frac{0.7059}{9.81} = 0.071957 \, \text{s}$$

2. Horizontal Dynamics ($x$-direction)

Total distance is the sum of airborne distance and sliding distance on the ground. From $\sum F = ma \Rightarrow a = -\mu g$:

$$s = v_0 \, t_{\text{air}} + \frac{v_0^2}{2\mu g}$$

Solving for $v_0$ with $s = 24 \, \text{in} = 0.6096 \, \text{m}$ and $\mu = 0.2$:

$$0.6096 = v_0(0.071957) + \frac{v_0^2}{2(0.2)(9.81)} \implies v_0 = 1.41 \, \text{m/s}$$

Energy required to launch the puck:

$$E = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(0.2268 \, \text{kg})(1.41)^2 = 0.225 \, \text{J}$$

3. Flywheel Geometry and Ideal Velocity

Center-of-mass velocity under a no-slip condition:

$$\omega_p = \frac{r\,\omega_m}{2r_p} \implies v_{cm} = \frac{r\,\omega_m}{2}$$

Wheel radius $r = 1.325 \, \text{in} = 0.033655 \, \text{m}$; contact arc $d_{\text{contact}} = \frac{\pi}{2} \cdot 1.325 \, \text{in} = 2.0813 \, \text{in}$.

Validated against test data ($r = 2.4 \, \text{in}$, $740 \, \text{RPM}$):

$$v_{cm} = \frac{(2.4 \times 0.0254)}{2} \times \frac{740 \cdot 2\pi}{60} = 2.36 \, \text{m/s}$$

4. System Efficiency and Motor Selection

Efficiency derived from experimental sliding data at $d = 12 \, \text{in}$, $\mu = 0.2$:

$$\eta = \frac{v_{\text{real}}}{v_{\text{ideal}}} = \frac{\sqrt{2(0.2)(9.81)(12 \times 0.0254)}}{2.36} = 0.4634 \quad (46.3\%)$$

Doubling range to $24 \, \text{in}$ requires $v_{\text{ideal}} \cdot \sqrt{2}$, and therefore $\text{RPM} \cdot \sqrt{2}$.

Final requirement: $\omega_{NL} \approx 1050{+} \, \text{RPM}$

Initial Design Test Results

Wheel size: 2.65 in  ·  Motor RPM: 742 (measured)  ·  Voltage: 33V

Distance achieved: 7.7 in

The transition from a small 3D-printed roller to a larger high-inertia flywheel was a critical pivot that solved the energy transfer issues inherent in firing heavy 8.0oz pucks. By exponentially increasing the wheel's moment of inertia, the system maintained its RPM during engagement, preventing the motor from stalling under load. This larger radius provided the necessary tangential velocity to hit the 1.41 m/s target speed, while the switch to a high-traction rubber surface ensured a no-slip condition, delivering the consistent power required to reliably reach the 8-foot scoring zone.

Puck Storage

We iterated through many designs of puck storage, including a ramp with doors and a conveyor belt, before settling on a revolving door mechanism for its mechanical reliability.

We used a servo (NEMA17) to rotate the "doors" of a circular storage unit. The unit is divided into four 90-degree chambers — three store pucks and one feeds to the ramp down to the flywheel.

The servo rotation is controlled by a timer set based on the time it takes for the flywheel to shoot a puck, and there is a 5-second timer for reloading pucks.
