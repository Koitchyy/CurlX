ME210 COMPETITION

Reflection

Drivetrain Integration

The greatest integration challenges arose when we tried to incorporate motion into our robot. I don't want to say that the process was traumatizing, but it was definitely the most time-consuming part of our integration and arguably the most influential factor in our final competition score, the main reason why we weren't able to advance past round two. Because of this mother-loving faulty drivetrain, my teammates and I grew gray hairs in the span of a week. So, future teams reading this, please, for the love of Tom, do not make these mistakes.

Motor Selection

The first thing that you have to take into consideration when designing the drivetrain of your robot is the torque and current rating of your motors and their power draw. Is it truly necessary to have motors on all your wheels, or can you execute the desired motion with fewer? The fewer motors you have, the fewer motor drivers you need, and the fewer pin connections you have. Therefore, the probability of a pin connection not being as tight as it needs to bereases! If your robot is considerably hefty, consider motors with a higher torque rating. At first, the CurlX team purchased higher RPM motors for the drivetrain, but promptly realized that having a bot that sprints away from you when set at a considerably low duty cycle is not ideal for the type of robot you are building in ME210. Even if you reduce the duty cycle of your PWM to make your motors run slower, if the RPM rating is too high and the torque rating is not high enough, there will be a point at which the robot will not move because it cannot overcome the force of the robot upon which it sits. In this class, you are probably dealing with a multi-sensor system that requires a certain time interval to tape sense, line follow, or read the signals of an IR beacon. If this is your case, which it probably will be, you need a robot with flexible speed control, so you can decrease speed when it needs to receive an electrical signal from the outside environment. Remember, flexibility is key in every single aspect of your design. If you leave yourself more leeway to experiment and adapt your design with the challenges that emerge along the way, you will save time debugging and money!

Mechanical Challenges

Motor Mounts & Shaft Couplers

More mechanical challenges emerged when dealing with the motor connection to the wheels. The CurlX team designed and 3D printed motor mounts and shaft couplers for the specific motor shaft. When integrating these parts with the motor, it is important to be cautious when tightening the screws that secure the motor mount. Overtightening these screws can increase friction in the gearbox and impede motor rotation.

Shaft Deflection

During drivetrain testing with the laser-cut chassis and mounted launching system, we noticed that the range of motion decreased with each test. After consulting with a TA, we realized that the shafts inside the motor and the shaft couplers were deflecting because they were supporting the weight of the entire robot. Since the wheels were not supported independently, the load was transferred through the shaft couplers connecting the motors to the wheels, causing the shafts to bend and interfere with smooth rotation.

Bearing Configuration

To avoid this issue and improve drivetrain stability, a revised system was designed using a single-bearing cantilevered configuration with roller pins beneath the middle of the platform to help distribute the load more evenly. In general, adding bearings to support the wheel shaft can significantly improve stability. A single bearing creates a cantilevered configuration where the wheel load produces a larger bending moment, increasing shaft deflection and vibration. Supporting the wheel with two bearings (one on each side) distributes the load, reduces bending moments, improves alignment, and results in a stiffer and more stable system.

As part of this redesign, we also implemented a more secure mounting method for the Mecanum wheels that allowed free rotation while preventing the full weight of the robot from being transferred through the shaft couplers. We strongly recommend that future teams implement this solution, as it significantly improves weight distribution and smoothness of motion while protecting the drivetrain's mechanical components from wear over time.

Final Recommendations

Weight Distribution & Duty Cycle Tuning

The weight of the robot should be distributed as evenly as possible across the four wheels so that each wheel contributes effectively to the robot's motion. We encountered several issues where three wheels carried most of the weight while one wheel spun freely in the air. While this might not significantly affect motion when using standard circular wheels, it becomes a major problem with Mecanum wheels, which rely on all four wheels working together to produce directional shifts, rotations about the axle, and overall movement. Thus, make sure the weight distribution of your platform is even among your wheels. If it can't be because of your design, which is what happened to us, we recommend tuning the duty cycle of each wheel so that the overall motion in each direction is m and the potential across the motor is the same. Although it took a lot of time and varied whether we were testing with our puck or not, we tuned the duty cycle for each wheel according to the weight on it in a pure experimental, empirical fashion, and it turned out to work very well. If possible, however, avoid this!

Motor Driver & Pin Connections

Finally, the issue that ultimately cost us points in the competition was the reliability of the motor driver and Arduino pin connections. Motor drivers can fail easily, and their electrical connections are often flimsy even when they appear to be properly connected. Before testing or competing, make sure these connections are securely fastened or, ideally, soldered to prevent intermittent failures. Since the drivetrain is critical to the robot's motion, following this advice will help ensure a more reliable and successful experience with the movement of your ME210 robot.
